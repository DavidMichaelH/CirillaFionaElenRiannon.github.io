An Upper Bound for the Hyperbolic Cosine Function

Theorem

Let \( x \in \mathbb{R} \). Then, the following inequality holds:

\( \cosh(x) \leq \exp \left ( \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right ) \).

Proof

We prove this by comparing the Taylor series expansions of \( \cosh(x) \) and \( \exp \left ( \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right) \). Recall that: \[ \cosh(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^{2k}}{(2k)!}, \quad \exp \left( \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^{2k}}{2^k k!}. \] Hence, comparing terms of the expansions, it suffices to show that for all \( k \), we have: \[ (2k)! \geq 2^k k!. \tag{1} \label{eq:ineq} \] First note that it holds in the case \(k=0\). Then for \(k \geq 1\), there is a simple way to see this by restricting to even terms in the factorial, \[ (2k)! = \prod_{i=1}^{2k} i \geq \prod_{i=1}^{k} (2i)= 2^k \prod_{i=1}^{k}i = 2^k k!. \]

Applying the inequality \(\ref{eq:ineq}\) to the Taylor series, we obtain: \[ \cosh(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{x^{2k}}{(2k)!} \leq \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{x^{2k}}{2^k k!} = \exp \left ( \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right ) . \]
