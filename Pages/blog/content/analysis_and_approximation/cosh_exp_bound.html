An Upper Bound for the Hyperbolic Cosine Function

Theorem

Let \( x \in \mathbb{R} \). Then, the following inequality holds:

\( \cosh(x) \leq \exp \left ( \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right ) \).

Proof

We prove this by comparing the Taylor series expansions of \( \cosh(x) \) and \( \exp \left ( \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right) \). Recall that: \[ \cosh(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^{2k}}{(2k)!}, \quad \exp \left( \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^{2k}}{2^k k!} \] It suffices to show that for all \( k \), we have: \[ (2k)! \geq 2^k k!. \] We prove this by induction.
Base Case: For \( k=0 \), we have \( (2\cdot 0)! = 1 \) and \( 2^0 0! = 1 \), so the inequality holds.
Inductive Step: Assume for some \( k \geq 0 \) that \[ 2^k k! \leq (2k)!. \] Then, for \( k+1 \): \[ 2^{k+1} (k+1)! = (2k+2) \cdot 2^k k! \leq (2k+2) \cdot (2k)! \] and since \( (2k+2)(2k+1)(2k)! = (2k+2)! \), it follows that: \[ 2^{k+1} (k+1)! \leq (2k+2)!. \] Thus, by induction, the inequality holds for all \( k \). Applying this inequality to the Taylor series, we obtain: \[ \cosh(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{x^{2k}}{(2k)!} \leq \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{x^{2k}}{2^k k!} = \exp \left ( \frac{1}{2} x^2 \right ) . \]
