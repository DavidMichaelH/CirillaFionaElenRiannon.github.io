Minimax and Bayes Risk in Statistical Estimation

Definition: Risk

In statistical decision theory, the risk of an estimator is the expected loss under a given loss function. Formally, if \( \theta \) is the parameter to be estimated, \( X \) is the data, the risk of an estimator \( \theta(X) \) is defined as: \[ R(\hat{\theta},\theta) = \mathbb{E} L(\hat{\theta}(X),\theta). \]

Example: Mean Squared Error

  • Mean Squared Error: \(L(x,y) = (x-y)^2\)
  • Mean Absolute Error: \(L(x,y) = |x-y|\)

Definition: Minimax Risk

In statistical decision theory, the minimax risk is the smallest possible maximum risk over all estimators. Formally, if \( R \) is risk function then: \[ \inf_{\hat{\theta}} \sup_{\theta} R(\hat{\theta}, \theta) \] An estimator \( \hat{\theta_0} \) is called a minimax estimator if it achieves this minimax risk, i.e., \[ \sup_{\theta} R(\hat{\theta_0},\theta ) = \inf_{\hat{\theta}} \sup_{\theta} R(\hat{\theta}, \theta) \]

Example: Minimax Estimator

Consider estimating the mean \( \theta \) of a normal distribution \( N(\theta, \sigma^2) \) with known variance \( \sigma^2 \). The sample mean \( \bar{X} \) is a minimax estimator under squared error loss because it minimizes the maximum risk over all possible values of \( \theta \). This is due to its constant risk function: \[ R(\theta, \bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \] which does not depend on \( \theta \).

Definition: Bayes Risk

The Bayes risk is the expected risk of an estimator with respect to a prior distribution \( \pi(\theta) \) over the parameter space. It is defined as: \[ r(\pi, \delta) = \int_{\Theta} R(\theta, \delta) \, \pi(\theta) \, d\theta \] An estimator \( \delta_{\pi} \) that minimizes the Bayes risk is called a Bayes estimator.

Example: Bayes Estimator

Suppose \( X \sim N(\theta, \sigma^2) \) and we place a prior distribution \( \theta \sim N(\mu_0, \tau^2) \) on \( \theta \). The Bayes estimator under squared error loss is the posterior mean: \[ \delta_{\pi}(X) = \frac{\tau^2}{\tau^2 + \sigma^2/n} \bar{X} + \frac{\sigma^2/n}{\tau^2 + \sigma^2/n} \mu_0 \] This estimator balances the sample information \( \bar{X} \) and the prior information \( \mu_0 \), weighted by their respective variances.
